# Non-embeddable Type II₁ Internal Algebras for the Spectral Standard Model

## Bottom line upfront

I did **not** find any published paper or arXiv preprint that actually replaces the finite internal algebra of the Connes–Chamseddine Standard Model spectral triple with a **non-embeddable Type II₁ factor** and then derives a corresponding gauge sector, Higgs sector, or chiral fermion spectrum. The literature instead falls into four adjacent clusters: the original finite-algebra Standard Model papers; finite-dimensional beyond-the-Standard-Model enlargements of that algebra; general Type II or semifinite spectral-triple theory; and MIP* = RE / Connes-embedding papers about non-approximable quantum correlations. Those clusters have not, in the published record I found, been fused into the specific construction you ask about. citeturn19view0turn20search0turn21search0turn41search0turn43search0turn35search1turn44view0turn30academia10turn28view0turn31search0

On the chirality question, the only **published** chirality content I found remains the usual one coming from the **finite KO-dimension-6 internal geometry** of the spectral Standard Model: the finite algebra is what fixes the Standard Model gauge group and the chiral fermion representations per generation. I found **no published II₁ or non-embeddable-II₁ extension** in which a new chiral spectrum is computed. citeturn19view0turn20search0turn21search0turn21search1

## Cited papers with summaries

1. **Alain Connes, 2006, _Noncommutative Geometry and the Standard Model with neutrino mixing_.**  
   This is one of the core papers fixing the modern finite-geometry form of the spectral Standard Model. Connes explicitly states that in the Standard Model geometry “all the ingredients are finite dimensional,” with the algebra given by the finite direct sum involving complex numbers, quaternions, and matrices, and he derives the Standard Model gauge action and a classification of admissible Dirac operators from that finite setup. For your question, this paper is important because it shows where the chirality content is actually coming from in the published model: from the finite real spectral triple of KO-dimension 6, not from any Type II factor. citeturn19view0

2. **Ali H. Chamseddine, Alain Connes, and Matilde Marcolli, 2006, _Gravity and the Standard Model with neutrino mixing_.**  
   This paper gives the canonical Connes–Chamseddine–Marcolli formulation of the Standard Model as a product of a four-dimensional spin manifold with a **finite-dimensional** noncommutative geometry \(F\). Its abstract and introduction are explicit that the discrete space \(F\) is finite-dimensional, metrically zero-dimensional, and responsible for the Standard Model gauge and Higgs structure. It is therefore one of the strongest pieces of evidence that the published particle-physics construction is built around a finite internal algebra, not a II₁ factor. citeturn20search0turn20search1

3. **Ali H. Chamseddine and Alain Connes, 2007, _Why the Standard Model_.**  
   This paper sharpens the classification story and argues that the finite real noncommutative geometry underlying the Standard Model is almost uniquely selected by the imposed axioms. It explicitly classifies **irreducible finite** geometries of the relevant KO-dimension and says that, once multiplicity and a quaternion-linearity assumption are imposed, the spectral action on \(M \times F\) delivers the Standard Model. In relation to your question, it reinforces that the published classification is a classification of **finite** internal geometries; it does not point toward a II₁-factor replacement. citeturn21search0turn21search4

4. **Ali H. Chamseddine and Alain Connes, 2007, _Conceptual Explanation for the Algebra in the Noncommutative Approach to the Standard Model_.**  
   This short paper is directly about why the algebra of the spectral Standard Model is the one it is. The abstract says the classification of irreducible geometries consistent with the reality and chiral conditions yields the Standard Model essentially uniquely, including the statement that there are **16 fermions per generation** with the correct representations and Higgs breaking pattern. For your chirality question, this is one of the key published sources: the chiral content is fixed in the literature by the finite algebra and KO-dimensional constraints, and I found no analogue of this classification for a II₁ internal algebra. citeturn21search1

5. **Ali H. Chamseddine, Alain Connes, and Walter D. van Suijlekom, 2013, _Beyond the Spectral Standard Model: Emergence of Pati-Salam Unification_.**  
   This is one of the nearest adjacent papers on the particle-physics side: it explicitly enlarges the internal algebraic data of the spectral Standard Model and studies the consequences for inner fluctuations and the scalar sector. But it still does so within the same almost-commutative paradigm, with a finite internal geometry and modified order-one/inner-fluctuation analysis, leading to a Pati–Salam-type gauge structure rather than any Type II factor. It is highly relevant because it shows that when Connes and collaborators did extend the internal algebra for model building, they stayed in the finite-dimensional arena. citeturn41search0

6. **Alain Connes and Henri Moscovici, 2006, _Type III and Spectral Triples_.**  
   This paper is the closest I found to a published statement by Connes about moving spectral-triple technology beyond the usual traceful type-I setting. It introduces twisted \(\sigma\)-spectral triples, explains why ordinary bounded commutators fail after certain noncentral perturbations, and replaces them with twisted commutators. It is extremely relevant as an adjacent mathematical direction, but it is **not** a Standard Model paper and it does **not** propose a Type II₁ internal algebra for the Standard Model. citeturn14view0

7. **Moulay-Tahar Benameur and Thierry Fack, 2006, _Type II non-commutative geometry. I. Dixmier trace in von Neumann algebras_.**  
   This paper is foundational for Type II spectral geometry. It defines what the authors call a Connes–von Neumann spectral triple, develops the associated index problem, and proves a local Hochschild-class formula using Dixmier traces in semifinite von Neumann algebras. For your question, it establishes that there is real published mathematics of spectral triples in the Type II world, but the context is general index theory and foliations, not the Standard Model and not non-embeddable II₁ particle physics. citeturn43search0turn43search4

8. **Alan L. Carey, John Phillips, Adam Rennie, and Fyodor A. Sukochev, 2006, _The Chern Character of Semifinite Spectral Triples_.**  
   This paper generalizes the odd and even local index formula to spectral triples for \(*\)-subalgebras of a general semifinite von Neumann algebra and explains how the resolvent cocycle represents the Chern character. In other words, it gives a large part of the Type II / semifinite local-index toolkit that one would need if one ever wanted a spectral-Standard-Model-like construction over a II₁ factor. But as published it remains a general index-theoretic development, not a particle model, and it says nothing about non-embeddability or Standard Model chirality. citeturn35search1

9. **Olivier Gabriel and Martin Grensing, 2013, _Ergodic Actions and Spectral Triples_.**  
   This paper is useful because it shows that some of Connes’ axioms do extend to infinite-dimensional \(C^*\)-algebra settings: for compact ergodic Lie-group actions it constructs real, finitely summable spectral triples satisfying the **first-order condition**. That matters for your obstruction question, because it shows the order-one/reality package is not intrinsically restricted to finite-dimensional internal algebras. However, the construction is not Type II₁-specific, not a Standard Model construction, and does not address chirality in the sense of the spectral Standard Model fermion spectrum. citeturn34view1

10. **Arup Chattopadhyay, Chandan Pradhan, and Anna Skripka, 2023, _Approximation of the Spectral Action Functional in the Case of \(\tau\)-Compact Resolvents_.**  
    This is one of the most relevant recent mathematical papers for your “does the spectral action survive?” question. It studies the spectral action functional \(V \mapsto \tau(f(H_0+V))\) in a **semifinite von Neumann algebra** setting with \(\tau\)-compact resolvent, and explicitly says it extends perturbation theory of the spectral action to that framework. This shows that there is real progress on a semifinite spectral action, but still not a published construction that feeds this into an almost-commutative Standard Model with a II₁ internal algebra, much less a non-embeddable one. citeturn44view0

11. **Sergio Doplicher and John E. Roberts, 1990, _Why there is a field algebra with a compact gauge group describing the superselection structure in particle physics_.**  
    This paper is relevant to your subfactor/operator-algebraic gauge-group question because it establishes, in algebraic quantum field theory, that compact gauge groups can be reconstructed from the superselection structure. It is the nearest classical operator-algebraic bridge from categorical data to continuous compact gauge symmetry in particle physics. But it is not phrased in the spectral-triple Standard Model language, does not use a Type II₁ internal algebra, and does not derive the Standard Model chiral spectrum from a factor inclusion. citeturn10search13turn10search1

12. **David E. Evans and Yasuyuki Kawahigashi, 2023, _Subfactors and Mathematical Physics_.**  
    This recent survey is the best concise source I found for what subfactor/planar-algebra technology actually gives. It explains that finite-index subfactors produce standard invariants; that finite depth corresponds to having finitely many irreducibles and hence a **fusion category**; and that planar algebras encode these structures. This is very relevant to your third question because it suggests a serious mismatch with the Standard Model gauge group, whose representation theory is not a finite fusion category. It therefore supports a cautious answer: subfactor theory gives powerful categorical machinery, but there is no published concrete inclusion \(N \subset M\) whose planar algebra is shown to realize the Standard Model gauge representations and chirality data in the Connes–Chamseddine sense. citeturn11view0turn12view1turn12view2

13. **Marius Junge, Miguel Navascués, Carlos Palazuelos, David Pérez-García, Volkher B. Scholz, and Reinhard F. Werner, 2010, _Connes’ embedding problem and Tsirelson’s problem_.**  
    This paper is the main pre-MIP* bridge between operator-algebra embeddability and physically motivated quantum-correlation questions. It shows that Tsirelson’s problem about tensor-product vs commuting-operator correlations is essentially equivalent to the Connes/Kirchberg finite-approximation problem, and it frames the issue explicitly in terms of algebraic QFT versus tensor-product quantum mechanics. For your question, it is highly relevant as background to why non-embeddable II₁ factors matter physically, but it is not about spectral triples or the Standard Model. citeturn27view3

14. **Zhengfeng Ji, Anand Natarajan, Thomas Vidick, John Wright, and Henry Yuen, 2020, _MIP* = RE_.**  
    This is the decisive source on the failure of Connes embeddability. The abstract explicitly states that the result implies undecidability for nonlocal games, a negative answer to Tsirelson’s problem, and—via the known equivalence machinery—a refutation of Connes’ embedding conjecture. This is the paper that makes non-embeddable II₁ factors unavoidable, but it does not connect them to spectral triples, the Standard Model, or chirality. citeturn30academia10

15. **Isaac Goldbring, 2021, _Non-embeddable II₁ factors resembling the hyperfinite II₁ factor_.**  
    This paper studies structural properties of non-embeddable II₁ factors after the collapse of the Connes embedding program. It is directly relevant because it makes clear that the post-MIP* literature did move on to study non-embeddable II₁ factors in earnest. Yet the focus is model theory and ultrapower properties, not noncommutative geometry model building. I found no passage from this line of work toward a Standard Model spectral triple. citeturn28view0

16. **Adán Cabello, Marco Túlio Quintino, and Matthias Kleinmann, 2023, _Logical possibilities for physics after MIP* = RE_.**  
    This is the cleanest physics-facing post-MIP* paper I found. It discusses the physical meaning of the existence of commuting-operator correlations that are not approximable by finite-dimensional tensor-product models and lays out possible ways physics could respond to that fact. It is useful evidence that MIP* = RE has indeed generated a physics discussion, but that discussion is about quantum correlations and the structure of physical theory, not about the Connes–Chamseddine Standard Model, internal spectral triples, or chirality. citeturn31search0

## What the literature supports

Across the core spectral-Standard-Model papers, the internal algebra stays finite-dimensional, and the published chirality content is tied to that finite KO-dimension-6 geometry. The finite-algebra classification papers are the ones that explain the Standard Model fermion representations, the hypercharge structure, and the Higgs mechanism in this framework. The closest beyond-Standard-Model papers by Connes and collaborators still enlarge the internal algebra only within the **finite** almost-commutative paradigm. I found no published paper by Connes, Chamseddine, or Marcolli that says: replace the finite internal algebra by a Type II₁ factor, much less by a **non-embeddable** Type II₁ factor, and derive a new chiral spectrum from it. citeturn19view0turn20search0turn21search0turn21search1turn41search0

The mathematical ingredients needed for such a project do exist **separately**. Type II and semifinite spectral triples are well developed in index-theoretic form, and some of the spectral-triple axioms and even spectral-action perturbation theory have been extended beyond the finite/type-I setting. So the obstruction is **not** that “Type II spectral triples do not exist.” The obstruction is that the particle-physics part of the Connes–Chamseddine construction—real even internal geometry, order-zero/order-one constraints, inner fluctuations giving the correct compact gauge group, anomaly-compatible chiral matter, and a workable spectral action—has **not** been assembled in the published literature for a II₁-factor internal algebra. The non-embeddable case is even farther away: the MIP* = RE papers show that such factors exist and matter for quantum correlations, but they do not furnish a spectral-triple prescription or a chiral particle spectrum. citeturn43search0turn35search1turn34view1turn44view0turn30academia10turn28view0turn31search0

On the subfactor side, the literature suggests a possible **categorical** foothold but not a finished candidate. Subfactor and planar-algebra technology naturally encodes rigid tensor categories and, in many tractable cases, fusion-category data. That makes it adjacent to gauge-representation questions. But the published subfactor literature I found does not provide a concrete inclusion \(N \subset M\) whose planar algebra has been shown to reproduce the Standard Model gauge group representations and chirality in the spectral-Standard-Model sense. The closest operator-algebraic route to compact gauge groups in particle physics remains the Doplicher–Roberts reconstruction program, which is conceptually neighboring but not the same construction. citeturn10search13turn11view0turn12view1turn12view2

## Limitations of the survey

This verdict is a **literature verdict**, not a theorem of nonexistence. I restricted attention to published papers and arXiv-posted preprints, as requested. Negative claims here mean: I did not find a direct construction or a direct published discussion in the literature surveyed, despite finding substantial adjacent work on finite-algebra spectral Standard Models, semifinite/Type II spectral triples, subfactor categories, and MIP* = RE physics consequences. citeturn19view0turn43search0turn11view0turn30academia10

## Overall verdict

My honest verdict is **(d) genuinely unexplored in the published/arXiv literature**, with one important nuance: the **surrounding ingredients are not unexplored**. The nearest adjacent published work on the **particle-physics side** is the finite-algebra extension program represented by the spectral Pati–Salam paper of Chamseddine, Connes, and van Suijlekom, which changes the internal algebra while staying almost-commutative and finite. The nearest adjacent published work on the **operator-algebra side** is the Type II / semifinite spectral-triple program of Benameur–Fack, Carey–Phillips–Rennie–Sukochev, and later semifinite spectral-action analysis such as Chattopadhyay–Pradhan–Skripka. To bridge from those to your target, one would need to develop, in a single framework, an **even real semifinite spectral triple over a II₁ factor with a KO-dimension-6 analogue**, a classification of its admissible Dirac operators and order-zero/order-one constraints, an inner-fluctuation mechanism yielding the correct compact gauge group and Higgs sector, and finally a derivation of an anomaly-compatible **chiral** fermion content. At present, I found no published work carrying out that bridge, and therefore no published answer to “what chirality emerges?” beyond the statement that the known chirality is still the usual finite-geometry Standard Model chirality. citeturn41search0turn43search0turn35search1turn44view0turn19view0turn21search1turn30academia10turn31search0
