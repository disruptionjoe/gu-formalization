# Literature review on spectral triples, anomaly classification, chirality no-go theorems, and distributed-systems analogies

## Executive summary

The strongest high-confidence finding is negative: I did **not** find any published paper or arXiv preprint that extends the Connes–Chamseddine Standard-Model spectral triple by replacing its finite internal algebra with a **non-embeddable Type II\(_1\)** factor and then derives the resulting gauge/Higgs sector or fermion chirality content. What **does** exist is a substantial adjacent literature on von Neumann and semifinite spectral triples, local index formulae in semifinite von Neumann algebras, and \(\tau\)-spectral-action functionals. That literature shows that pieces of the analytic machinery survive beyond finite-dimensional algebras, but it stops well short of a Standard-Model construction, and I found no published computation of emergent SM-like chirality for a II\(_1\) internal algebra—embeddable or non-embeddable. The nearest algebraic candidates from subfactor theory are subgroup-subfactors and \(\mathrm{SU}(3)\)-flavored \(A_2\)-planar algebras, but I found no paper connecting them to a Connes–Chamseddine internal algebra for the SM. citeturn10view0turn13search1turn10view2turn10view3turn10view4turn42search2turn42search9turn8search2turn8search1

On the Freed–Hopkins side, the literature consistently formulates anomaly classification in terms of **symmetry type, tangential structure, locality/functoriality, invertibility, and reflection positivity/unitarity**—not in terms of an observer model. I found no published variant of the Freed–Hopkins classification explicitly recast for relational QM, QBism, quantum reference frames, superdeterminism, or IIT-style observers. The nearest adjacent work falls into two buckets: first, internal extensions of the formalism to relative QFTs, higher-form symmetries, noninvertible/categorical symmetries, and crystalline symmetries; second, a separate observer/reference-frame literature in QFT and quantum foundations that does not link back to cobordism classification. My verdict is therefore that “Freed–Hopkins is observer-substrate-pairing-relative” is **not established** and, in the published/arXiv literature I found, is best classified as **genuinely unexplored** rather than an active named conjecture. citeturn19view0turn19view1turn19view2turn20search2turn20search4turn19view3turn19view4turn23search0turn23search4turn23search11turn19view5turn22view0

For the four no-go theorems, the literature strongly supports an “assumptions matter” reading, but with an important asymmetry. The Witten 1981/1983 Kaluza–Klein chirality obstruction and the Nielsen–Ninomiya doubling theorem have many well-known circumventions that work **by dropping a hypothesis**: orbifolds, boundaries, background gauge fields, branes, non-Killing/internal backgrounds, extra dimensions, domain-wall fermions, Ginsparg–Wilson/overlap fermions, Floquet topology, and related constructions. By contrast, the Freed–Hopkins anomaly/cobordism program is less a single impossibility theorem than a classification theorem with a sharply delimited domain of validity; the “evasions” here are mostly extensions that drop invertibility or enlarge the symmetry notion. The Distler–Garibaldi result remains robust **inside** its hypothesis class; successful bypasses come from leaving the “single \(E_8\) with the SM/gravity embedded in it” framework, especially via string/F-theory constructions with localized matter or enhancement points rather than a single adjoint-\(E_8\) centralizer story. citeturn25view0turn25view1turn26search7turn26search12turn28search8turn27search1turn32search2turn37view3turn36search1turn36search2turn37view0turn37view1turn19view0turn20search2turn20search1turn16search1turn40view0turn38search10turn38search2turn38search6

For the distributed-systems question, the literature I found is again mostly negative. I did **not** find a formal published analogy mapping FLP to Witten 1981, CAP to Freed–Hopkins, or BFT thresholds to chirality/anomaly no-go theorems. What I did find are adjacent literatures: discrete/computational reformulations of fermion-doubling problems in Floquet/QCA settings; “consensus between observers” in quantum Darwinism; and quantum-reference-frame/QFT work on observer dependence. Those are real and relevant neighbors, but they do **not** amount to a formal FLP/CAP/BFT-style recasting of the physics no-go theorems named in your prompt. My verdict is therefore that the explicit analogy is, as of the literature I could locate, **genuinely unexplored**. citeturn37view0turn37view1turn39search3turn23search0turn39search1

## Connes–Chamseddine, Type II₁ internal algebras, and chirality

A useful way to read this literature is to separate three layers: the **actual Standard-Model spectral triple** in the Connes–Chamseddine tradition; the **broader noncommutative-geometry machinery** for von Neumann or semifinite algebras; and the **operator-algebra / subfactor / MIP\(^*\)** literature that makes non-embeddable II\(_1\) factors mathematically unavoidable. What is missing is the bridge paper that combines all three. citeturn10view0turn13search1turn10view2turn10view3turn8search2

### Cited works and summaries

1. **2007 — Ali H. Chamseddine, Alain Connes, Matilde Marcolli, “Gravity and the Standard Model with Neutrino Mixing.”** This is the canonical Connes–Chamseddine–Marcolli SM construction in which the internal geometry is still a **finite** real spectral triple. The source I opened summarizes the key point plainly: the finite geometry has algebra \(A_F=\mathbb C\oplus\mathbb H\oplus M_3(\mathbb C)\), finite Hilbert space, and finite Dirac operator, with the KO-dimension/chirality structure carefully tuned to solve the fermion-doubling problem and include neutrino mixing. For your question, this paper is important mainly because it marks the starting point: the published SM model is built on a finite-dimensional internal algebra, not a II\(_1\) factor. citeturn10view0

2. **2007 — Ali H. Chamseddine, Alain Connes, “Conceptual Explanation for the Algebra in the Noncommutative Approach to the Standard Model.”** Chamseddine and Connes explicitly say they assume spacetime is the product of a four-manifold with a **finite noncommutative space** \(F\), and they classify irreducible geometries \(F\) under reality and chirality constraints to recover the Standard Model data. This matters because the paper narrows, rather than broadens, the internal algebra class: it explains why the finite algebra is conceptually natural inside the Connes–Chamseddine program, but it offers no move toward type II factors, embeddable or non-embeddable. citeturn13search1

3. **1997 — Thomas Krajewski, “Classification of finite spectral triples.”** Krajewski’s paper is one of the core finite-dimensional classification results underlying the almost-commutative SM program. It is directly relevant because it shows how deeply the standard model construction is rooted in the **finite** real-even-spectral-triple setting. In other words, the available SM-model-building toolkit in this line of work is not merely “currently finite”; it is structurally organized around finite spectral triples and their Krajewski diagrams, so moving to a II\(_1\) internal factor would require a different classification technology. citeturn9search4

4. **2000 — James E. McCarthy, Emil J. Schrohe, Jinsung Yang, “On von Neumann spectral triples.”** This paper is one of the cleanest adjacent results on the operator-algebra side. It defines the notion of a **von Neumann spectral triple** and studies its Chern–Connes character and local formula. Its relevance is that it demonstrates that spectral-triple technology is not restricted to finite/type-I settings. But it is an index-theoretic extension, not a particle-physics construction: it gives formal machinery for von Neumann-algebraic spectral triples, not a Standard-Model internal algebra, and certainly not a non-embeddable II\(_1\) replacement for \(A_F\). citeturn10view2

5. **2004 — Alan L. Carey, John Phillips, Adam Rennie, Fyodor A. Sukochev, “The Local Index Formula in Semifinite von Neumann Algebras I: Spectral Flow.”** Carey–Phillips–Rennie–Sukochev generalize the Connes–Moscovici local index formula to spectral triples for a *-subalgebra of a **general semifinite von Neumann algebra**. This is one of the strongest pieces of evidence that the analytic backbone of NCG can survive beyond finite/internal-matrix algebras. For your question, however, it is still only an adjacent enabling result: nothing in the paper constructs a Connes–Chamseddine Standard Model with a II\(_1\) internal factor, and nothing in it computes the resulting left/right fermion content. citeturn10view3

6. **2023 — Arup Chattopadhyay, Chandan Pradhan, Anna Skripka, “Approximation of the spectral action functional in the case of \(\tau\)-compact resolvents.”** This paper is important for the specific obstruction question about the **spectral action**. It studies the functional \(V\mapsto \tau(f(H_0+V))\) for self-adjoint operators with \(\tau\)-compact resolvent in a **semifinite von Neumann algebra**. So the answer to “does some spectral-action-like principle survive beyond finite algebras?” is analytically **yes, in semifinite trace form**. But the paper is not a particle-physics model: it does not derive SM gauge fields, Higgs fields, or chirality from a II\(_1\) internal algebra. It is therefore a necessary adjacent ingredient, not the sought extension itself. citeturn10view4

7. **2015 — Francesco D’Andrea, Ludwik Dąbrowski, “The Standard Model in Noncommutative Geometry and Property (M).”** The lines I retrieved are telling: even within the ordinary finite SM triple, D’Andrea and Dąbrowski show that property (M)—a spinorial/Clifford-type strengthening—**fails for the original Chamseddine–Connes triple unless one modifies the Dirac operator and grading**, with possible new fields as a consequence. For your question this is crucial negative evidence: if chirality/spinorial consistency is already delicate in the finite internal algebra, there is presently no published road from that finite analysis to a II\(_1\)-factor internal geometry with a controlled SM-like chiral spectrum. citeturn10view1

8. **2020 and 2023 — Z. Ji, A. Natarajan, T. Vidick, J. Wright, H. Yuen, “MIP\(^*\)=RE”; Adán Cabello, Marco Túlio Quintino, Matthias Kleinmann, “Possible consequences for physics of the negative resolution of Tsirelson’s problem.”** Ji et al. established MIP\(^*\)=RE, which implies a negative answer to Tsirelson/Connes-embedding-type approximation questions in the commuting-operator setting. Cabello–Quintino–Kleinmann then discuss possible physical consequences of that negative resolution. These papers are directly relevant to your question about non-embeddable II\(_1\) factors being physically meaningful, but only at the level of **quantum correlations and foundational modeling**. I did not find, in these or nearby papers, a link to Standard-Model spectral triples or to emergent chirality from a non-embeddable II\(_1\) internal algebra. citeturn8search2turn8search1

9. **2008 — Ved Prakash Gupta, “Planar Algebra of the Subgroup-Subfactor”; 2009 — David E. Evans, Mathew Pugh, “A\(_2\)-Planar Algebras I” and “A\(_2\)-Planar Algebras II: Planar Modules.”** These papers are the clearest “subfactor-theory candidate” neighbors I found. Gupta treats subgroup-subfactors \(R\rtimes H\subset R\rtimes G\), while Evans–Pugh develop \(A_2\)-planar algebras and relate them to **\(\mathrm{SU}(3)\)** subfactors and modular invariants. This means subfactor theory does offer algebraic environments that encode finite-group symmetries and \(\mathrm{SU}(3)\)-flavored representation data. But I found no paper using such inclusions/planar algebras as the internal algebraic sector of a Connes–Chamseddine Standard-Model triple, and none extracting the full \(\mathrm{SU}(3)\times\mathrm{SU}(2)\times\mathrm U(1)\) SM gauge content in that way. citeturn42search9turn42search2turn42search5turn42search8

10. **2025 — David E. Evans, Yasuyuki Kawahigashi, “Subfactors and Mathematical Physics”; 2025 — “von Neumann Subfactors and Non-invertible Symmetries.”** These papers show that the subfactor world is now deeply entangled with modern mathematical physics, including QFT and generalized symmetry. They strengthen the case that subfactors are physically meaningful algebraic objects, not merely abstract operator algebra. Still, they do not close the gap to your target: I found no published construction taking a non-embeddable II\(_1\) factor from this world and turning it into the internal Connes–Chamseddine algebra of the Standard Model. citeturn42search4turn42search1

### Verdict

My verdict on the specific proposal—“extend the Connes–Chamseddine Standard-Model spectral triple to a **non-embeddable Type II\(_1\)** internal algebra and determine the resulting chirality content”—is **genuinely unexplored in the published/arXiv literature I found**. The nearest adjacent published work is the combination of (i) semifinite/von-Neumann spectral triple theory and local index/spectral-action machinery, and (ii) the ordinary finite-algebra Standard-Model triple. Bridging them would require, at minimum, a real-even II\(_1\) internal spectral triple with a faithful trace, a replacement for the finite classification of internal Dirac operators, a proof that order-zero/order-one/orientability/Poincaré-duality-type conditions still make physical sense, and an explicit extraction of gauge/Higgs/fermion representations. At present I found no paper that performs that bridge, and no paper that computes SM-like left/right chirality or generation structure from such an internal II\(_1\) factor. citeturn13search1turn10view0turn10view2turn10view3turn10view4turn10view1turn8search2

## Freed–Hopkins, anomalies, and observer-substrate pairing

The core interpretive distinction here is between what the Freed–Hopkins program **explicitly assumes**—symmetry type, manifolds with extra structure, invertibility, locality/functoriality, reflection positivity—and what it does **not** say much about, namely the metaphysics of the observer. The literature I found is very clear on the former and nearly silent on the latter. citeturn19view0turn19view2turn20search4

### Cited works and summaries

1. **2014 — Daniel S. Freed, “Anomalies and Invertible Field Theories.”** Freed’s paper gives the modern geometric viewpoint in which anomalies are encoded by invertible field theories, and anomalous theories are naturally “relative” to those anomaly theories. It is foundational for the whole program, but it is framed in terms of QFT geometry and projectivity, not observer classes. In particular, the paper treats anomalies as structural features of functorial QFT data, not as relative to Copenhagen, QBist, relational, or superdeterministic observer models. citeturn19view2turn17search8

2. **2012/2014 — Daniel S. Freed, Constantin Teleman, “Relative quantum field theory.”** Freed–Teleman is important because it introduces **relativity** in a technical sense—an anomalous or boundary theory can be relative to a higher-dimensional theory—but this is not “observer relativity.” It is relativity to ambient field-theoretic data. The paper is therefore the nearest internal analogue of your “pairing-relative” question, but it does not address observer-substrate pairings; it gives a categorical notion of relativity internal to QFT. citeturn15search2turn15search9

3. **2016/2021 — Daniel S. Freed, Michael J. Hopkins, “Reflection positivity and invertible topological phases.”** This is the central cobordism-classification paper. The abstract states the inputs clearly: invertible topological QFTs, extended reflection positivity, and stable homotopy/cobordism methods. For condensed-matter applications, the paper also assumes the existence and validity of low-energy effective field theory approximations of lattice systems. In other words, its hypothesis class is about **unitarity/reflection positivity, symmetry, and locality**, not about an observer model. citeturn19view0turn16search2

4. **2023 — Daniel S. Freed, “What is an anomaly?”** This paper sharpens the interpretation further by defining the anomaly of a quantum field theory as an expression of its **projective nature**, leading to a once-categorified invertible theory. Again, the stress is on projectivity and higher structure. This clarifies the philosophy of the anomaly program, but the philosophy is about quantum theory as projective/functorial—not about classical observers, smooth measurement postulates, or observer-substrate pairing invariance. citeturn21search16turn20search5

5. **2021 — Arun Debray, “Invertible phases for mixed spatial symmetries and the fermionic crystalline equivalence principle.”** Debray is one of the strongest pieces of adjacent evidence for **frame/space dependence inside the formalism**. He explicitly states that Freed–Hopkins give an ansatz for invertible phases with spatial symmetry, and then generalizes it to cases where symmetry type mixes nontrivially with spatial symmetry. This shows that the formalism is sensitive to the **symmetry/background structure chosen**, especially crystalline/spatial structure. But that is still very different from dependence on an observer interpretation of quantum mechanics. citeturn19view3turn41view1

6. **2018/2019 — Dominic V. Else, Ryan Thorngren, “Crystalline topological phases as defect networks.”** Else–Thorngren give a physical picture of crystalline topological phases as defect networks on a space \(X\) with a symmetry-group action. This directly supports the claim that topological-phase classification can depend on **spatial symmetry and embedding data**. It does **not** imply observer dependence, but it does show that the relevant background structures are not always purely internal group symmetries. That is a meaningful adjacent notion of “pairing to a substrate,” though still not the observer-theoretic notion in your prompt. citeturn19view4

7. **2022/2024 — Daniel S. Freed, Gregory W. Moore, Constantin Teleman, “Topological symmetry in quantum field theory”; 2024 — Shi Chen, “Anomaly and invertible field theory with higher-form symmetry: Extended group cohomology”; 2018/2019 — Zheyan Wan, Juven Wang, “Higher anomalies, higher symmetries, and cobordisms I.”** These works are the main published extensions of the Freed–Hopkins program beyond ordinary invertible/0-form symmetry settings. Freed–Moore–Teleman treat topological, noninvertible, and categorical symmetries; Chen extends the algebraic/cohomological side to higher-form symmetry; Wan–Wang generalize cobordism classification to higher symmetries and higher anomalies. Together they show that the classification is **actively extended in symmetry space**, but none of the papers I found reformulates it in relational/QBist/QRF/superdeterministic observer terms. citeturn20search2turn20search6turn20search1turn16search1turn16search15

8. **2024–2026 adjacent observer-framework papers — Christopher Fewster et al., “Quantum reference frames, measurement schemes and quantum fields”; Jan Głowacki, “Towards Relational Quantum Field Theory”; S. Fedida et al., “Foundations of Relational Quantum Field Theory I: Scalars.”** These papers show that there is now a genuine literature on **observer/reference-frame relativity in QFT**, including quantum reference frames and relational QFT. They are precisely the kind of literature one would have to connect to Freed–Hopkins if one wanted an observer-pairing-relative anomaly classification. But in the published papers I found, that connection has not yet been made. citeturn23search0turn23search4turn23search11

9. **2023–2025 adjacent topological-phase papers — Wei-Bin Pan et al., “Weyl semimetal from non-inertial observers”; Lin Su et al., “Topological Phase Transitions and Mixed State Order in a Hubbard Quantum Simulator.”** These papers are not part of the Freed–Hopkins program, but they matter for parts of your subquestions. Pan et al. argue that a non-inertial observer can effectively see a topologically nontrivial Weyl semimetal where an inertial observer saw a trivial massive Dirac fermion; Su et al. argue that certain mixed-state topological criticalities depend on the observer’s information. Both are strong adjacent evidence that **reference frame** or **observer information** can matter for topological phenomena. But neither paper recasts Freed–Hopkins anomaly/cobordism in those terms. citeturn19view5turn22view0

### Verdict

My verdict is **genuinely unexplored**. The published Freed–Hopkins corpus I found neither asserts observer-pairing invariance nor develops observer-pairing relativity. It classifies anomaly/invertible-field-theory data relative to **symmetry type, tangential structure, and locality/unitarity hypotheses**, and nearby extensions modify those ingredients—not the observer model. The nearest adjacent published work that would need to be extended is the conjunction of Freed–Teleman relative QFT, Debray’s work on spatially mixed symmetries/local systems, and the emerging QRF/relational-QFT literature. A real bridge would need to promote “observer/reference frame” into the symmetry/background data of the field theory, or else reinterpret observer choice as a relative/parametrized structure in the bordism target. I found no paper that does this. citeturn19view1turn19view3turn23search0turn23search4turn23search11turn19view0

## Chirality and anomaly no-go theorems

### Witten’s Kaluza–Klein chirality obstruction

Witten’s 1981 and 1983 papers target the problem of obtaining left-right asymmetric 4D fermion quantum numbers from higher-dimensional theories compactified on smooth compact spaces. The strongest version of the obstruction in the retrieved text is explicitly tied to the case where **all gauge fields arise from the metric/isometries** and there are **no elementary gauge fields**: under those assumptions, Witten states that an Atiyah–Hirzebruch theorem implies the higher-dimensional Dirac operator gives only **vector-like** 4D fermion quantum numbers. His 1981 paper already states the phenomenological conclusion in softer language: one can get an \(\mathrm{SU}(3)\times\mathrm{SU}(2)\times\mathrm U(1)\) gauge group, but the proper fermion quantum numbers are hard to obtain. citeturn25view0turn25view1turn41view0

1. **1983 — Edward Witten, “Fermion Quantum Numbers in Kaluza–Klein Theory.”** This is the most direct statement of the chirality obstruction I retrieved. The abstract says that in the absence of elementary gauge fields, Atiyah–Hirzebruch implies vector-like quantum numbers in 4D, and Witten also shows a similar obstruction for the Rarita–Schwinger operator on homogeneous spaces. As a hypothesis list, the paper is best read as assuming: smooth compactification, ordinary higher-dimensional Dirac/Rarita–Schwinger operators, and gauge interactions coming from geometry/isometries rather than independent higher-dimensional gauge bundles. citeturn25view0turn41view0

2. **1981 — Edward Witten, “Search for a Realistic Kaluza–Klein Theory.”** The 1981 paper is the original physics statement that 11D supergravity compactification can yield the SM gauge group but not realistic chiral fermion quantum numbers. Relative to the 1983 paper, it is less a theorem-proof article and more the phenomenological problem statement. It is still indispensable because it defines the target: smooth Kaluza–Klein geometry was hoped to unify gauge interactions and gravity, but realistic chirality was the stumbling block. citeturn25view1

3. **1996 — Petr Hořava, Edward Witten, “Eleven-Dimensional Supergravity on a Manifold with Boundary.”** Hořava–Witten is a classic and successful way around the original smooth, boundaryless compactification picture. By putting 11D supergravity on a manifold with boundary and coupling boundary gauge fields/anomaly inflow, it drops the assumption of a closed smooth internal compactification without localized gauge sectors. This is not a direct “proof of chiral generations in 4D SM” paper, but it is one of the historically decisive ways the old Kaluza–Klein obstruction was bypassed in string/M-theory model building. **Verdict:** successful as a bypass of Witten’s original hypothesis class; not a direct recovery of the literal original KK program. citeturn26search7

4. **2000 — Howard Georgi, Andrew K. Grant, Girma Hailu, “Chiral fermions, orbifolds, scalars and fat branes.”** This paper gives a very clean orbifold/boundary-condition bypass: chiral fermion zero modes are produced by orbifold boundary conditions in higher-dimensional theories, with localization on “fat branes.” It drops the smooth-manifold assumption in favor of orbifold fixed points/boundaries. **Verdict:** successful as an evasion by singular/boundary compactification, though not a derivation of the full Standard Model from pure smooth KK geometry. citeturn26search12

5. **2015 — Wilfried Buchmüller et al., “Chiral fermions and anomaly cancellation on orbifolds with flux.”** Buchmüller and collaborators explicitly state that **orbifold singularities and flux lead to chiral fermions in four dimensions**. This is a strong modern example of how the chirality obstruction is bypassed by simultaneously dropping smoothness and allowing background flux/gauge structure. **Verdict:** successful for generating 4D chirality in the broader compactification/string-model-building sense; not a rescue of Witten’s restricted pure-geometric KK assumptions. citeturn28search8

6. **2025 — João Baptista, “Chiral interactions of fermions and massive gauge fields in Kaluza–Klein models.”** Baptista is especially relevant to your “dropping hypotheses” framing, because the abstract explicitly says the paper questions the usual assumption that SM gauge fields must come from **exact Killing vector fields** of the internal space. The claim is that gauge fields linked to non-Killing internal fields can induce asymmetric couplings of left- and right-handed fermions and thereby circumvent traditional no-go arguments. **Verdict:** an intriguing and directly on-point recent evasion attempt, but still **open** from the perspective of producing a full realistic SM chirality sector. citeturn27search1turn27search5

A specific negative result from my literature search is that I did **not** locate published/arXiv papers explicitly recasting Witten’s Kaluza–Klein chirality obstruction in the non-deterministic/probabilistic, causal-set, or Wolfram-hypergraph-rewriting terms mentioned in your prompt. I therefore treat those subdirections as unsubstantiated by the literature I found, not as inactive because of a theorem.  

### Nielsen–Ninomiya fermion doubling

The retrieved sources characterize the theorem by a familiar bundle of assumptions: **translation invariance**, **locality/short range or regularity of the Fourier transform**, **Hermiticity**, and a local conserved charge or a particular realization of chiral symmetry. The sources I retrieved also make clear that the common modern lesson is not “no chiral fermions on the lattice, period,” but “not while keeping all of those assumptions simultaneously.” citeturn32search2turn32search0turn36search2turn37view0

1. **1981 — H. B. Nielsen, M. Ninomiya, “A no-go theorem for regularizing chiral fermions.”** The original theorem states the impossibility in very general regularization language and is the baseline against which all later evasions are measured. The search result I retrieved also lists the usual assumption package: locality, Hermiticity, translation symmetry, and related regularity conditions in momentum space. citeturn32search2turn32search6turn32search0

2. **1992 — David B. Kaplan, “A Method for Simulating Chiral Fermions on the Lattice.”** Kaplan’s domain-wall construction is one of the canonical successful evasions. It drops the assumption that everything lives in the original even-dimensional lattice theory by introducing an extra dimension and localizing zero modes on a topological defect. The abstract explicitly says doublers may be decoupled in a gauge-invariant manner and flavor anomalies observed on a finite lattice. **Verdict:** successful and foundational for vectorlike lattice theories and for chiral-gauge-theory attempts, though mirror-fermion issues remain subtle in fully chiral gauge theories. citeturn37view3turn36search12turn36search16

3. **1997/1998 — Herbert Neuberger, “Exactly massless quarks on the lattice” and “More about exactly massless quarks on the lattice.”** Neuberger’s overlap construction is the other canonical success story. The 1997 abstract states there is **no undesired doubling and no fine tuning** for strictly massless quarks, using the overlap operator built from the Wilson kernel. **Verdict:** successful for exact massless fermions in the vectorlike case; the key point is that it changes the realization of chiral symmetry rather than preserving the naive one. citeturn36search1turn36search5turn36search13

4. **1998 — Martin Lüscher, “Exact chiral symmetry on the lattice and the Ginsparg–Wilson relation.”** Lüscher makes the logical status of the evasion explicit: the Nielsen–Ninomiya contradiction is avoided because the chiral symmetry is realized in a **different way** than was assumed in the theorem. This paper is the cleanest place to mark the dropped assumption: exact continuum-style onsite chirality is replaced by Ginsparg–Wilson lattice chirality. **Verdict:** successful conceptual evasion and the standard explanation of why overlap/domain-wall fermions do not contradict Nielsen–Ninomiya. citeturn36search2

5. **2010 — Michael Creutz, “Classification of Minimally Doubled Fermions” and related minimal-doubling work.** Minimal-doubling constructions keep locality and an exact chiral symmetry but pay by breaking other lattice symmetries, such as full hypercubic symmetry, and still retain **two** species rather than a single Weyl fermion. **Verdict:** partial evasion only. It weakens doubling but does not fully achieve the single-chiral-fermion dream. citeturn35search0turn35search10turn35search12

6. **2021 — Takumi Bessho, Masatoshi Sato, “Nielsen–Ninomiya Theorem with Bulk Topology: Duality in Floquet and Non-Hermitian Systems.”** Bessho–Sato show that once one leaves the original **static** setting and allows bulk topology intrinsic to periodically driven or non-Hermitian systems, the original no-go constraint changes: bulk chiral fermions become possible in the dynamical setting they analyze. **Verdict:** successful as a theorem-level evasion by dropping staticity/Hermiticity assumptions, though it is not a standard local-Hamiltonian continuum regulator for the SM. citeturn37view0

7. **2025 — Doğukan Bakırcıoğlu, Pablo Arnault, Pablo Arrighi, “Fermion Doubling in Quantum Cellular Automaton Models”; 2015 — J. Dubail, N. Read, “Tensor network trial states for chiral topological phases in two dimensions and a no-go theorem in any dimension.”** The QCA paper shows that fermion doubling persists in discrete-time computational models and proposes a covering-map “flavoring-without-staggering” fix that coexists with Nielsen–Ninomiya rather than simply violating it; Dubail–Read show that tensor-network trial states can represent chiral free-fermion phases, but any strictly short-range quadratic parent Hamiltonian is gapless. **Verdict:** QCA gives an interesting computationally flavored open direction rather than a settled solution; tensor-network/chiral-PEPS work succeeds at representation but typically fails to recover a strictly short-range gapped parent Hamiltonian, so it is best classified as a **different-obstruction** outcome, not a complete escape. citeturn37view1turn33search10turn37view2

A further nuance is that staggered fermions are not a full evasion: they reduce doubling and encode anomaly information in a more subtle lattice symmetry structure, but they do not yield a single chiral Weyl fermion. Recent work on quantized axial charge in staggered fermions emphasizes precisely that the anomaly matching remains consistent with Nielsen–Ninomiya rather than defeating it. citeturn35search7turn36search7

### Freed–Hopkins anomaly and cobordism classification

The “no-go theorem” label is slightly misleading here. What the literature actually gives is a classification of anomalies/invertible phases under a definite hypothesis class: **invertible**, **functorial/local**, usually **reflection-positive/unitary**, with a specified **symmetry/tangential structure**, and typically interpreted as the low-energy EFT of a gapped phase or anomaly inflow system. The right way to ask for evasions is therefore: what happens when one drops invertibility, enlarges the symmetry notion, weakens unitarity/reflection positivity, or changes the tangential/background data? citeturn19view0turn19view2turn20search4turn19view3

1. **2016/2021 — Daniel S. Freed, Michael J. Hopkins, “Reflection positivity and invertible topological phases.”** This is the theorem-level benchmark. It explicitly implements extended reflection positivity and computes deformation classes via homotopy theory/cobordism spectra. The main hypotheses are plainly the ones listed above. citeturn19view0turn16search2

2. **2012/2014 — Daniel S. Freed, Constantin Teleman, “Relative quantum field theory.”** Relative QFT is not an evasion in the sense of defeating the theorem, but it does weaken the demand that a theory be absolute/standalone. An anomalous theory can be consistent as a boundary/relative theory even when it is not a standalone, anomaly-free one. **Verdict:** successful extension of the formalism, but internal to the same categorical worldview. citeturn15search2turn15search9

3. **2022/2024 — Daniel S. Freed, Gregory W. Moore, Constantin Teleman, “Topological symmetry in quantum field theory.”** This paper moves beyond ordinary invertible symmetries to noninvertible/categorical/topological symmetries. In your “drop a hypothesis” language, it drops the tacit assumption that relevant symmetry data are exhausted by ordinary groups and invertible defects. **Verdict:** a successful and active extension beyond the original symmetry class, but not an observer-theoretic evasion. citeturn20search2turn20search6

4. **2018/2019 — Zheyan Wan, Juven Wang, “Higher anomalies, higher symmetries, and cobordisms I”; 2024 — Shi Chen, “Anomaly and invertible field theory with higher-form symmetry: Extended group cohomology.”** These papers enlarge the framework to higher-group/higher-form symmetries. They show that once the symmetry input is broadened, the anomaly classification broadens accordingly. **Verdict:** successful extensions by dropping the ordinary-0-form-symmetry restriction; again, not an observer-framework modification. citeturn16search1turn16search15turn20search1

5. **2021 — Arun Debray, “Invertible phases for mixed spatial symmetries and the fermionic crystalline equivalence principle.”** Debray weakens the simplicity of the background data by allowing local systems of symmetry types that mix nontrivially with spatial symmetry. This is a real published refinement of the formalism’s “background-class assumption.” **Verdict:** successful extension in the spatial/crystalline direction, but still no observer-model dependence. citeturn19view3turn41view1

For your specifically requested “non-standard observer framework” evasions, my answer is negative: I did **not** find published/arXiv work that reformulates the Freed–Hopkins anomaly classification for relational QM, QBism, quantum reference frames, superdeterminism, or IIT-like observers. The nearest adjacent literature is on QRF/QFT and relational QFT, not on anomaly cobordism.  

### Distler–Garibaldi on \(E_8\)

The Distler–Garibaldi theorem is very tightly framed. The paper analyzes subgroups of real and complex forms of \(E_8\) and concludes that any attempt to embed gravity plus the SM into a real or complex \(E_8\) in the intended “theory of everything inside \(E_8\)” style fails certain representation-theoretic tests required by physics. So the core hypotheses are: a **single** real or complex \(E_8\), a representation-theoretic embedding of the relevant gauge/Lorentz data into it, and realistic chirality/generation content extracted from that embedding. citeturn40view0

1. **2009/2010 — Jacques Distler, Skip Garibaldi, “There is no ‘Theory of Everything’ inside \(E_8\).”** This is the theorem. The abstract is already enough to state the hypothesis class and conclusion: the obstruction is representation-theoretic and applies to the “everything inside one \(E_8\)” program. It is not a blanket statement against all uses of \(E_8\) in string theory or geometry. citeturn40view0

2. **2009 — Jonathan J. Heckman, Alireza Tavanfar, Cumrun Vafa, “The Point of \(E_8\) in F-theory GUTs.”** This is one of the clearest published bypasses by hypothesis-dropping. The paper uses a **point of \(E_8\) enhancement in internal geometry** to organize Yukawa couplings and flavor structure in F-theory GUTs. That is crucially different from requiring the entire 4D theory—including all generations and gravity—to sit inside one adjoint \(E_8\) representation in the Distler–Garibaldi sense. **Verdict:** successful bypass of the theorem’s hypothesis class. citeturn38search10

3. **2009 — Emilian Dudas, Eran Palti, “Froggatt–Nielsen models from \(E_8\) in F-theory GUTs.”** Dudas–Palti likewise use \(E_8\) enhancement in F-theory as local geometric input for flavor structure, with three generations arising from different matter curves. Again, this is a way of using \(E_8\) that lies outside the Distler–Garibaldi setup. **Verdict:** successful bypass of the single-\(E_8\)-adjoint-centralizer vision, though of course not a proof of a unique exact SM vacuum. citeturn38search2

4. **2024 — Fernando Marchesano et al., “The Standard Model from String Theory.”** This review is useful because it locates the modern consensus: three-generation chiral Standard-Model-like sectors arise in string compactifications through mechanisms such as localized matter, fluxes, intersecting objects, and enhancement points—including \(E_8\)-related structures in F-theory—rather than through a single finite-dimensional \(E_8\) adjoint representation. **Verdict:** successful broad bypass by moving to string-compactification frameworks, not by defeating Distler–Garibaldi on its own ground. citeturn38search6

5. **2024–2025 — Robert Arnott Wilson, “On possible embeddings of the Standard Models of Particle Physics and Gravity in \(E_8\)” and “Embeddings of the Standard Model in \(E_8\).”** These recent arXiv preprints show that attempts to revive or modify \(E_8\)-embedding stories continue. But in the literature I found, they are not established consensus answers to Distler–Garibaldi, and they do not overturn the theorem as stated. **Verdict:** open/speculative attempts, not recognized successful evasions of the theorem’s original hypothesis class. citeturn38search9turn38search1turn38search12

### Cross-pattern analysis

The four theorem families line up surprisingly well once one writes them in “assumption-decomposition” form. The table below summarizes the shared structure.

| Witten Kaluza–Klein | Smooth compact internal space; gauge fields from isometries/metric; no elementary gauge fields; ordinary Dirac/Rarita–Schwinger compactification | Orbifold singularities, background flux/gauge bundles, boundaries, branes, non-Killing internal fields | Many successful bypasses **outside** original hypothesis class citeturn25view0turn26search7turn26search12turn28search8turn27search1 |

The recurring pattern is that all four results become most powerful when a problem is squeezed into a **highly rigid representational class**: smooth compact bundles, local translationally invariant lattices, invertible/functorial QFT with fixed symmetry type, or a single encompassing Lie algebra representation. The successful evasions nearly always relax one of those rigidities by adding singularities, higher categorical structure, extra dimensions, localized sectors, modified symmetry realizations, or different substrates. I did **not** find a published paper that explicitly unifies all four theorem families under the single slogan in your prompt—“forgetful images of substrate-level invariants in the smooth bundle shadow.” The closest verdict I can justify is that this framing is **implicit/adjacent**, not explicit, in the existing literature. citeturn25view0turn36search2turn19view0turn40view0

## Physics no-go theorems and distributed-systems impossibility

The direct literature here is sparse to the point of near-absence. ### Cited works and summaries

1. **2021 — Takumi Bessho, Masatoshi Sato, “Nielsen–Ninomiya Theorem with Bulk Topology: Duality in Floquet and Non-Hermitian Systems.”** This is the closest thing I found to a “distributed-systems-style” recasting of a physics no-go theorem, because it treats the no-go as dependent on the *system model*—static versus dynamical, Hermitian versus non-Hermitian—and shows that changing the model changes the impossibility statement.
