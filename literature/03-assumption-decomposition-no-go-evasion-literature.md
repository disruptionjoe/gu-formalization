# Assumption Decomposition and Evasion Literature for Four Chirality and Anomaly No-Go Theorems

## Witten Chirality Obstruction in Smooth Kaluza-Klein Reduction

What is explicit in the published theorem-class, as it is used in later literature, is narrower than some of the meta-assumptions in your prompt. The original 1981 paper studies realistic higher-dimensional Kaluza–Klein / 11d supergravity compactification and emphasizes the difficulty of obtaining the correct fermion quantum numbers from smooth compactification. In later M-theory restatements, this becomes the clean slogan that **chiral fermions do not arise when the internal manifold \(X\) is smooth**, so the obstruction is specifically tied to a **smooth compact internal geometry**, rather than to orbifolds, boundaries, singular loci, or topological defects. Published presentations of this no-go do **not** formulate it in terms of determinism, observer protocol, partial-order causality, or probabilistic reduction. citeturn14search0turn14search1turn32view0

1. **1983 — S. Randjbar-Daemi, A. Salam, J. Strathdee, *Instanton Induced Compactification and Fermion Chirality*.**  
   This is one of the earliest genuine ways around the smooth-geometry obstruction. The paper asks directly about fermion chirality in Kaluza–Klein theories **with Yang–Mills coupling** and uses nontrivial gauge backgrounds / instantons in the compact space to obtain chirality. In assumption language, it drops the idea that the internal sector is only a smooth gravitational compactification with trivial gauge background. **Verdict:** succeeds as an evasion of the simplest smooth-pure-geometry version, but it is a chirality mechanism rather than a complete Standard Model construction. citeturn34search2turn34search6turn40search8

2. **1996 — Petr Hořava, Edward Witten, *Eleven-Dimensional Supergravity on a Manifold with Boundary*.**  
   Hořava–Witten changes the class completely: the internal space is no longer a closed smooth compact manifold of the older Freund–Rubin type, but a manifold **with boundary**, and gauge/anomaly data already enter at the boundary. The paper shows that anomaly cancellation fixes an \(E_8\) gauge group on each boundary component. This does not yet give a 4d Standard Model, but it is a canonical evasion of the old smooth-compactification class by adding boundary degrees of freedom. **Verdict:** successful as a class-level evasion of the smooth-without-boundary setup; not by itself a full realistic chiral compactification. citeturn41search3

3. **2000 — Howard Georgi, Aaron K. Grant, Girma Hailu, *Chiral fermions, orbifolds, scalars and fat branes*.**  
   This paper is a standard orbifold / brane-localization evasion. Its starting point is explicit: **orbifold boundary conditions** in extra dimensions produce **chiral fermion zero modes**, and those modes can localize on “fat branes.” The theorem-class being dropped is smooth compactification without singular/orbifold boundary identification. **Verdict:** succeeds as a mechanism for obtaining chiral zero modes in higher-dimensional field theory; still not a full UV-complete Standard Model. citeturn35search0turn35search5

4. **2004 — Bogdan A. Dobrescu, Eduardo Pontón, *Chiral Compactification on a Square*.**  
   This is one of the clearest “twisted boundary condition” realizations in the literature. The paper identifies side-pairings of a square with phases and shows that the resulting compactification allows a **chiral fermion content in four dimensions**; it further proves that the allowed phases make the construction equivalent to a \(T^2/\mathbb Z_4\) orbifold. In assumption language, it drops smooth manifold compactification in favor of **orbifolded / phase-twisted boundary data**. **Verdict:** succeeds for chirality generation in extra-dimensional EFT. citeturn17search10turn41search2

5. **2001 — Bobby S. Acharya, Edward Witten, *Chiral Fermions from Manifolds of \(G_2\) Holonomy*.**  
   This is the cleanest singularity-based answer to the smoothness obstruction. The abstract states the key point with unusual directness: compactification on a \(G_2\)-holonomy manifold gives chiral fermions in four dimensions **only if \(X\) is singular**. This is not a loophole inside the old theorem; it is an explicit statement that the theorem is escaped by leaving the smooth category. **Verdict:** successful evasion by dropping smoothness. citeturn15search0

6. **2003 — Bobby S. Acharya, Frederik Denef, Chris Hofman, N. Lambert, *Freund–Rubin Revisited*.**  
   This paper is especially relevant because it rephrases the old result in exactly the terms you are after: it says the old Freund–Rubin program “failed due to Witten’s no go theorem which states that chiral fermions do not arise when \(X\) is smooth,” and then shows that **Freund–Rubin vacua with singularities** can in fact contain chiral fermions. It also gives a useful cautionary lesson: some intersecting-brane / singular constructions produce a chiral fermion and its charge-conjugate partner, so they evade smoothness but still fail to yield a net chiral spectrum. **Verdict:** succeeds as a singular-space evasion; some concrete realizations fail at the “net chirality” stage rather than at smoothness. citeturn32view0

The pattern in the established literature is therefore very sharp: **published successful evasions of Witten 1981 are geometric**—background gauge flux, orbifolds, boundaries, branes, or singularities. In the corpus reviewed for this report, I did **not** locate a published or arXiv-standard line of work treating Witten’s chirality obstruction by explicitly dropping determinism, adopting a measure-theoretic reduction, moving to causal-set partial orders, or using Wolfram-style hypergraph rewriting as the central evasion mechanism.

## Nielsen-Ninomiya Doubling and Its Workarounds

The explicit hypotheses are much more concrete here. In modern summaries of the original theorem, the obstruction applies to **free/bilinear lattice fermions** with **exact lattice \(U(1)_V\)** and **\(U(1)_A\)** symmetries, together with **lattice translation invariance** and suitable continuity assumptions on the spectrum. More general modern formulations isolate **locality, Hermiticity, translation invariance, and conserved on-site charges with discrete spectra**. Later work makes explicit that the original theorem assumes no interactions, while the deeper obstruction is anomaly-theoretic. Again, the published theorem is not stated in terms of observer model, stochasticity, or partial-order time. citeturn24view0turn22search15turn23search3

1. **1998 — S. V. Zenkin, *Extension of the Nielsen-Ninomiya theorem*.**  
   This paper is important because it shuts down one tempting evasion route. Rather than escaping the theorem by dropping translation invariance or locality, Zenkin argues that the no-go extends to **translation non-invariant and non-local** formulations using the index theorem. In assumption language, the paper says that removing translational symmetry by itself is not enough. **Verdict:** fails as an evasion; it strengthens the obstruction. citeturn7search13

2. **1992 — David B. Kaplan, *A Method for Simulating Chiral Fermions on the Lattice*.**  
   Kaplan’s domain-wall construction is the classic successful workaround. It moves the problem into **one higher dimension** and realizes chiral zeromodes on a **topological defect / boundary**, with doublers decoupled in a gauge-invariant way. This drops the assumption that the chiral theory must live as a strictly intrinsic \(d\)-dimensional local lattice fermion system. **Verdict:** successful as an extra-dimensional boundary workaround; it became one of the standard routes around doubling. citeturn35search2

3. **1997 — Herbert Neuberger, *Exactly massless quarks on the lattice*.**  
   Neuberger’s overlap formulation gives a doubler-free realization of exactly massless quarks on the lattice and explicitly states that there is **no undesired doubling and no fine tuning**. The price is that one no longer uses the naive continuum form of lattice chiral symmetry; the overlap operator implements chirality in a more subtle way linked to the Ginsparg–Wilson relation. **Verdict:** successful for vector-like theories and foundational for later chiral formulations, though it does not by itself solve every non-Abelian chiral gauge-theory problem. citeturn36search1turn36search5

4. **1998 — Martin Lüscher, *Exact chiral symmetry on the lattice and the Ginsparg-Wilson relation*.**  
   Lüscher states the logical core of the evasion with unusual clarity: the Ginsparg–Wilson relation yields an **exact lattice symmetry**, and there is no contradiction with Nielsen–Ninomiya because **the symmetry is realized in a different way than assumed in the theorem**. This is the clean example of evading a no-go by dropping not locality or Hermiticity, but the **naive on-site realization of chiral symmetry**. **Verdict:** successful modified-symmetry evasion. citeturn37search0

5. **2013 — T. B. Wahl, H.-H. Tu, N. Schuch, J. I. Cirac, *Projected Entangled-Pair States Can Describe Chiral Topological States*.**  
   This is the main tensor-network result adjacent to the doubling problem. The paper shows that PEPS can indeed describe chiral topological states, but only with a cost: the exact parent Hamiltonians are either **local and gapless** or **gapped with power-law-decaying hopping**, and the authors derive conditions showing that free-fermionic chiral PEPS cannot be exact ground states of **gapped local** parent Hamiltonians. **Verdict:** partial success; chiral phases are representable, but the locality/gap obstruction reappears in a different guise. citeturn35search3

6. **2024 — Evan Berkowitz, Aleksey Cherman, Tom Jacobson, *Exact lattice chiral symmetry in 2d gauge theory*.**  
   This paper is one of the clearest recent low-dimensional successes. It constructs **symmetry-preserving lattice regularizations** of 2d QED and of the **3450 chiral gauge theory**, using bosonization and Villain-type lattice actions, with the internal global symmetries acting locally and the anomalies reproduced already at finite lattice spacing. In assumption language, it leaves the traditional free-fermion lattice class and moves to a more strongly bosonized / dual lattice description. **Verdict:** succeeds in \(1+1\) dimensions for anomaly-free chiral gauge theories; it is not yet a settled \(3+1\)d solution. citeturn23search8turn23search14

There are also several more specialized branches worth separating from the main line. **Dropping Hermiticity** gives proof-of-principle counterexamples with serious physical costs: Chernodub’s 2017 PT-symmetric non-Hermitian model can realize an unequal number of left- and right-handed massless modes, but the PT symmetry is spontaneously broken and the spectrum acquires complex branches. **Staggered-fermion** work, such as Chatterjee–Pace–Shao 2024, does not overthrow Nielsen–Ninomiya; instead it realizes lattice charges whose noncommutativity matches the continuum anomaly in a way the authors say is **consistent** with the theorem. And the closest thing I found to your asynchronous-CA request is the discrete-time **QCA** paper of Bakircioglu–Arnault–Arrighi 2025, which fixes fermion doubling in QCA by a flavoring construction while explicitly explaining why the method **coexists with**, rather than refutes, Nielsen–Ninomiya. citeturn22search0turn38search1turn39search1

The honest state of the field is therefore mixed. **Some workarounds are fully successful inside their intended class**—domain walls, overlap/GW fermions, and now some \(1+1\)d anomaly-free lattice constructions. But the most ambitious target, a completely satisfactory **local \(3+1\)d chiral gauge regularization** of Standard Model type, remains an active program rather than a closed problem. citeturn35search2turn37search0turn23search8turn30search7

## Freed-Hopkins Cobordism Classification and What It Does Not Assume

Strictly speaking, Freed–Hopkins is not a no-go theorem in the same style as Witten 1981 or Nielsen–Ninomiya 1981. The core published result classifies **deformation classes of reflection-positive, invertible, extended field theories with fixed symmetry type** using generalized cohomology / bordism methods, and its application to lattice phases is made under the explicit assumption that a **low-energy effective field theory approximation exists and is valid**. Freed’s earlier anomaly paper and later extended-functorial refinements frame anomalies as **invertible / relative field theories in one higher dimension**. In the literature I reviewed, the adjustable input is symmetry type, tangential structure, extension level, and invertibility—not an observer model, measurement protocol, QBist agency, relational observer, or quantum reference-frame choice. citeturn42view0turn42view1turn26search3turn10search12

1. **2014 — Daniel S. Freed, *Anomalies and Invertible Field Theories*.**  
   This is the conceptual foundation. Freed argues that an anomalous QFT is best understood as a **relative** field theory attached to an **invertible** anomaly theory in one higher dimension. The paper is not an evasion attempt; it establishes the class assumptions that later cobordism classifications formalize. **Verdict:** theorem-setting background, not a loophole. citeturn26search3turn10search7

2. **2016 / 2021 — Daniel S. Freed, Michael J. Hopkins, *Reflection positivity and invertible topological phases*.**  
   This is the flagship classification result. The abstract makes the assumptions explicit: **extended reflection positivity** for **invertible topological QFTs**, stable-homotopy computation of deformation classes, and application to lattice systems only **assuming** valid low-energy EFT. This is the paper that fixes the relevant class, rather than evading it. **Verdict:** establishes the classification; no observer-relative variant is introduced. citeturn42view0

3. **2023 — Daniel Grady, *Deformation classes of invertible field theories and the Freed–Hopkins conjecture*.**  
   Grady proves the Freed–Hopkins conjecture relating **reflection-positive, invertible, \(d\)-dimensional extended field theories with fixed symmetry type** to generalized cohomology of a Thom spectrum. This is mathematically important because it confirms that the bordism/cohomology description is not merely heuristic. **Verdict:** reinforces the theorem-class rather than opening a loophole. citeturn42view1

4. **2021 — Arun Debray, *Invertible phases for mixed spatial symmetries and the fermionic crystalline equivalence principle*.**  
   Debray explicitly presents his work as a **generalization** of the Freed–Hopkins ansatz, now allowing spatial symmetry to mix nontrivially with fermion parity. This is highly relevant to your “assumption dropping” criterion because it shows a real extension of the framework when one changes the **symmetry/background class**—but the extension remains fully inside the same functorial-invertible-cobordism paradigm. **Verdict:** successful extension of symmetry assumptions; not an observer-based evasion. citeturn26search9

5. **2017 / 2018 — Juven Wang, Xiao-Gang Wen, Edward Witten, *Symmetric Gapped Interfaces of SPT and SET States: Systematic Constructions*.**  
   This paper gives the cleanest published route around “anomaly forbids a symmetric trivial boundary.” Its key move is **symmetry extension**: an anomalous \(G\)-symmetry on the boundary becomes non-anomalous when lifted to an extended symmetry \(H\), and the boundary may then be a **topologically ordered gauge theory** rather than an invertible boundary. So the dropped assumption is not observer structure; it is the assumption that the boundary must remain **invertible / trivially gapped**. **Verdict:** succeeds when one allows topological order and symmetry extension. citeturn29search0

6. **2019 — Clay Córdova, Kantaro Ohmori, Shu-Heng Shao, *Anomaly Obstructions to Symmetry Preserving Gapped Phases*.**  
   This paper sharply limits how far the above maneuver can go. It identifies an obstruction in the anomaly inflow action that must vanish for a symmetry-preserving **gapped phase**, i.e. a unitary TQFT boundary, to exist; otherwise the system must be **gapless** or **spontaneously break symmetry**. In effect, it shows that “drop invertibility and add topological order” is not a universal escape hatch. **Verdict:** many evasion attempts fail at a different obstruction. citeturn29search5

7. **2024 — Anton Kapustin, Nikita Sopenko, *Anomalous symmetries of quantum spin chains and a generalization of the Lieb-Schultz-Mattis theorem*.**  
   This is the closest published work I found to a “drop some microscopic assumptions and ask what survives” program. Kapustin and Sopenko define an anomaly index for **locality-preserving** group actions on spin chains and prove that a nonzero anomaly forbids any invariant Hamiltonian from having invariant gapped ground states. This matters because it shows that even after leaving the original clean fermion-band setting, anomaly obstructions often persist. **Verdict:** dropping translation or going to spin systems with locality-preserving tails does not generally evade the obstruction. citeturn30search0

The crucial bottom line for your earlier observer-framework question is negative: in the corpus reviewed here, I did **not** find a published or arXiv-standard literature that reformulates Freed–Hopkins in explicitly **relational-QM, QBist, quantum-reference-frame, superdeterministic, or IIT-style observer** terms. The closest adjacent papers modify **symmetry**, **spatial background**, **invertibility**, or **microscopic substrate**—for example crystalline symmetry, mixed spatial symmetry, average symmetry under disorder, or topologically ordered boundaries—but not observer/substrate pairing as a formal parameter. citeturn26search9turn9search14turn27search0

## Distler-Garibaldi and the Single-E8 Obstruction

Here the assumptions are unusually explicit because Distler and Garibaldi name them. One fixes a real Lie group \(E\) with subgroups \(SL(2,\mathbb C)\) and \(G\) such that **\(G\) is connected, compact, and centralizes \(SL(2,\mathbb C)\)** (ToE1); in the \(SL(2,\mathbb C)\times G\) decomposition of \(\mathrm{Lie}(E)\), one imposes **\(V_{m,n}=0\) for \(m+n>4\)**, excluding exotic higher-spin matter (ToE2); and one requires **\(V_{2,1}\)** to be a **complex representation of \(G\)**, encoding chirality (ToE3). Their theorem is then that there are **no** such ToE subgroups in complex \(E_8\) or any real form of \(E_8\). This theorem is representation-theoretic, not an anomaly theorem, and it does not assume smoothness, discretization, or observer class. citeturn20view0

1. **2010 — A. Garrett Lisi, Lee Smolin, Simone Speziale, *Unification of gravity, gauge fields, and Higgs bosons*.**  
   This is a direct continuation of the E8-valued-connection strategy that Distler–Garibaldi target. The paper proposes a unification framework in which gravity and gauge fields are packaged in a connection valued in a larger Lie algebra, with \(E_8\)-type ambitions still central. Because Distler–Garibaldi’s theorem was designed precisely against this class of “single \(E_8\), Lorentz plus compact gauge subgroup” constructions, it is best read as a response that remains **inside** the obstructed class. **Verdict:** fails as an evasion if one keeps the Distler–Garibaldi hypotheses. citeturn11search5turn20view0

2. **2009 / 2010 — Fabrizio Nesti, Roberto Percacci, *Chirality in unified theories of gravity*.**  
   Nesti and Percacci drop the single-\(E_8\) premise and move to the “GraviGUT” group \(SO(3,11)\). They show how to obtain **a single chiral \(SO(10)\) family** starting from a Majorana–Weyl representation of the larger group. This is a textbook example of evading a no-go by leaving its group-theoretic class. **Verdict:** successful chirality construction after dropping the single-\(E_8\) assumption, but it is only one family and not a complete three-generation SM+gravity theory. citeturn12search0

3. **2005 — Volker Braun, Yang-Hui He, Burt A. Ovrut, Tony Pantev, *A Heterotic Standard Model*.**  
   This is one of the strongest “outside the theorem’s class” successes. The paper constructs realistic \(N=1\) vacua from **\(E_8\times E_8\)** heterotic compactification on a smooth Calabi–Yau threefold with a nontrivial gauge bundle, obtaining gauge group \(SU(3)_C\times SU(2)_L\times U(1)_Y\times U(1)_{B-L}\) and **three families** of quarks and leptons. What is dropped is exactly what Distler–Garibaldi require: a **single** \(E_8\), with the SM emerging from one 4d representation-theoretic centralizer story. **Verdict:** successful broader construction. citeturn11search6

4. **2005 / 2006 — Volker Braun, Yang-Hui He, Burt A. Ovrut, Tony Pantev, *The Exact MSSM Spectrum from String Theory*.**  
   This paper strengthens the previous point: it explicitly advertises the **exact MSSM spectrum** from \(E_8\times E_8\) heterotic string compactified on a suitable Calabi–Yau threefold with gauge instanton and Wilson line data. This is not a loophole inside Distler–Garibaldi; it is a demonstration that once one leaves the single-\(E_8\), one-step, four-dimensional adjoint picture, realistic chirality is possible. **Verdict:** successful broader-class evasion. citeturn11search2

5. **2014 / 2015 — Lara B. Anderson et al., *\(E_8\times E_8\) heterotic Calabi–Yau models based on flux breaking of the visible \(E_8\) group down to the Standard Model group, without an intermediate GUT and Wilson lines*.**  
   Anderson and collaborators show that the visible \(E_8\) can be broken directly to the Standard Model group in \(E_8\times E_8\) heterotic compactifications using flux breaking, without passing through an intermediate GUT. This again succeeds precisely by moving away from Distler–Garibaldi’s one-\(E_8\), compact-centralizer, adjoint-decomposition setting toward string compactification with bundle data. **Verdict:** successful direct-SM heterotic route outside the theorem’s class. citeturn18search11

6. **2025 — Krzysztof A. Meissner, Hermann Nicolai, *Standard Model Symmetries and \(K(E_{10})\).* **  
   This is an example of a more speculative beyond-\(E_8\) move: the paper embeds Standard Model symmetries into a quotient of the maximal compact subgroup \(K(E_{10})\) and advertises the correct charge and chiral assignments for all fermions. Because the symmetry object is now an **infinite-dimensional Kac–Moody** structure, this definitely leaves the Distler–Garibaldi class. But this is still far from a fully demonstrated realistic theory. **Verdict:** open and speculative; promising only as a beyond-finite-dimensional-group direction. citeturn18search10

So Distler–Garibaldi behaves differently from the first three cases. The theorem itself appears robust **within its stated class**. Published “evasions” that genuinely work do so by abandoning that class—most notably via **\(E_8\times E_8\) string compactifications** or by moving to **different unification groups**—not by finding a loophole inside single-\(E_8\) representation theory. In the corpus reviewed here, I did not find a strong published counterexample that keeps all of ToE1–ToE3 and still reproduces three chiral SM generations plus gravity inside a single real form of \(E_8\).

## Shared Assumption Patterns Across the Four No-Go Results

There **is** a common pattern, but it is only partly explicit in the literature. Witten 1981 blocks chirality in the class of **smooth compact internal geometry**; Nielsen–Ninomiya blocks chirality in the class of **local, Hermitian, translation-invariant lattice fermion systems with exact charge structure**; Freed–Hopkins classifies anomalies inside the class of **reflection-positive, invertible, extended field theories with fixed symmetry type**; and Distler–Garibaldi blocks unification in the class of **single-\(E_8\), compact-centralizer, low-spin, chiral representation theory**. In each case, the no-go is not “everything is impossible,” but “everything is impossible **inside a sharply delimited class**.” citeturn32view0turn24view0turn42view0turn20view0

The most visible cross-theorem escape pattern is a move to **larger substrate data**. Witten is evaded by adding **boundaries, orbifolds, singularities, or branes**. Nielsen–Ninomiya is evaded by adding a **higher-dimensional bulk** (domain walls), changing the symmetry realization (GW/overlap), or moving to nonstandard dual formulations. Freed–Hopkins obstructions are commonly bypassed only if one allows the boundary to carry **topological order** or a **symmetry extension** instead of requiring an invertible trivial boundary. Distler–Garibaldi is evaded only by moving to **\(E_8\times E_8\) bundles/compactifications** or to **other symmetry groups**. So the common architectural move is: when the theorem obstructs one “shadow” description, successful work usually **adds hidden bulk, boundary, singular, bundle, or higher-symmetry structure**. citeturn41search3turn35search2turn29search0turn11search6turn12search0

There is also a partial topological unification of the first three no-gos. Modern work increasingly reads Nielsen–Ninomiya as an anomaly statement rather than merely a band-structure accident, and Freed–Hopkins makes anomaly classification fully cohomological/cobordic. Witten’s chirality obstruction is not itself presented in cobordism language, but many of its successful evasions are likewise mediated by singularities and anomaly inflow. Distler–Garibaldi is the outlier: its obstruction is representation-theoretic, and the literature does not naturally fold it into the same anomaly/cobordism package. citeturn24view0turn30search0turn26search3turn15search4turn20view0

That is why the specific framing you propose—**“all four no-gos are forgetful images of substrate-level invariants in the smooth bundle shadow”**—looks to me **implicit/adjacent for the first three, but genuinely original once Distler–Garibaldi is folded into the same meta-picture**. I did not find a paper in the cited corpus that explicitly unifies **all four** in that language, or one that treats observer class, determinism, and substrate type as a single common axis across all four theorems.

## Overall Verdict

My bottom-line assessment is as follows. **Witten 1981** is very much a theorem about a **smooth compactification class**, and the literature contains several established evasions—orbifolds, boundaries, branes, flux backgrounds, and singularities—so that obstruction is **not** absolute outside the smooth category. **Nielsen–Ninomiya** has a large and mature evasion literature; some routes are clearly successful in their intended setting (domain walls, overlap/GW fermions, some \(1+1\)d anomaly-free constructions), while others just move the obstruction or pay a cost in locality, gap structure, symmetry realization, or physicality. **Freed–Hopkins** is different: the published literature mostly extends or applies the classification, or circumvents boundary obstructions by allowing topological order or symmetry extension; I found **no** comparable literature making it observer-relative in the sense of relational/QBist/reference-frame pairings. **Distler–Garibaldi** remains solid inside its stated class, and the successful neighboring constructions all work by leaving that class, most convincingly through \(E_8\times E_8\) heterotic compactifications or entirely different unification groups. citeturn32view0turn35search2turn37search0turn23search8turn42view0turn29search0turn20view0turn11search6turn12search0

So if I map your final choices onto the literature I reviewed: the claim that there is a **shared class-assumption signature** running through the four no-gos is **well supported in an adjacent sense**; the stronger claim that the four no-gos are explicitly treated in the literature as **forgetful images of deeper substrate-level invariants** is, in my judgment, **not explicit**. The nearest truthful verdict is therefore: **the framing is implicit/adjacent for Witten–Nielsen–Ninomiya–Freed–Hopkins, but largely original once Distler–Garibaldi is integrated into the same meta-structure.**
